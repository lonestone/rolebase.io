A horizontal organization, sometimes called a "flat structure," is a management model characterized by an equal distribution of responsibilities and influence within the company. Its goal is to foster autonomy and collective decision-making among organization members.

The idea of a horizontal organization is based on rejecting the traditional hierarchy, where power is concentrated at the top and decisions flow from the top down. Instead, in a flat structure, each organization member has an equal voice and an equal share of responsibility. This creates an environment where collaboration and active participation are encouraged.

A horizontal organization can be particularly attractive for companies that seek to cultivate a democratic work climate and leverage the skills and ideas of all employees. It is often associated with participatory decision-making methods, such as consensus or consent, which allow the entire organization to contribute to defining objectives and solving problems.

While a horizontal organization can offer many advantages, it also comes with challenges. It can sometimes be difficult to implement in large companies, and conflict management can become more complex without a traditional hierarchical structure.

In summary, a horizontal organization is a management model that promotes equal responsibilities and influence within the company. It aims to encourage autonomy and collective decision-making, and is particularly suited to organizations that wish to cultivate a culture of collaboration and active engagement among their members. However, its implementation can be challenging and may require adaptation depending on the size and complexity of the organization.
