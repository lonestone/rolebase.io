## 1. Build trust and set clear expectations

Trust is the cornerstone of constructive exchanges between colleagues. Without it, even the most well-meaning advice risks being misinterpreted and perceived as criticism. In environments where trust is strong, employees report reduced stress, increased energy, better productivity, and greater engagement.

### Create a psychologically safe environment

Psychological safety means being able to speak freely without fearing repercussions or humiliation. Yet, according to a 2019 [Gallup](https://www.gallup.com/home.aspx) survey, only 3 out of 10 employees believe their opinion is truly taken into account at work. To foster this climate, managers and teams must adopt respectful behaviors, such as active listening, and turn mistakes into learning opportunities.

### Define clear mutual expectations

Once trust is established, it is crucial to clarify expectations. This involves an open discussion about goals, boundaries, and each person's role. This approach helps every team member understand exactly what is expected of them and how their work fits into the overall mission. Documenting these expectations in writing can serve as a shared reference point and strengthen coordination.

### Encourage transparent communication

An article in the [Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/) highlights that respect is the primary factor driving employee engagement. Transparency, in turn, relies on honest sharing of information and the ability to ask questions without fear of judgment. Leaders play a key role by leading by example: admitting their mistakes and showing vulnerability inspires others to be more open during exchanges.

These elements (trust, clear expectations, and open communication) are essential for getting the most out of collaborative tools and organized methods, and for optimizing feedback among colleagues.

## 2. Use the Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI) model

Once trust is established, it is essential to organize your feedback in a clear and structured way. The SBI model is a powerful tool for achieving this, by breaking each piece of feedback into three elements: the situation, the behavior, and the impact.

### The three key elements of the SBI model

**The situation**: It provides context. For example, instead of saying "you're often late," be specific: "last Tuesday, during the 9:00 AM team meeting." This precision avoids generalizations and reduces the risk of defensive reactions.

**The behavior**: Here, focus on observable facts. Rather than saying "you seemed disinterested," describe objectively: "you were frequently checking your phone and did not participate in the discussion." By staying factual, you maintain an objective and constructive exchange.

**The impact**: Explain the concrete consequences of the behavior using personal statements. For example: "this gave the impression that you were not fully engaged, which made it harder for the team to benefit from your ideas."

### Examples of using the SBI model

For **positive feedback** after a client presentation:

- **Situation**: "During last week's client presentation..."

- **Behavior**: "...you presented the timeline clearly and answered questions with confidence..."

- **Impact**: "...which strengthened the client's trust in our team."

For **constructive feedback** about a task management delay:

- **Situation**: "Last Friday, during the report finalization..."

- **Behavior**: "...you submitted your section two hours past the deadline..."

- **Impact**: "...which delayed the team's work and created additional stress."

These examples show how the SBI model enables precise and useful feedback, while opening the door to concrete improvements.

### Tips for successful implementation

- **Prepare in advance**: Identify the three elements (situation, behavior, impact) before the conversation. This will help you stay factual and prevent emotions from disrupting the exchange.

- **Choose the right moment**: Give your feedback shortly after the event, ideally within 24 to 48 hours, when memories are still fresh but emotions have settled.

- **Adopt a professional and neutral tone**: The goal is to encourage growth and improvement, not to criticize. This fosters open dialogue and limits resistance.

By using the SBI model, you transform potentially delicate conversations into moments of learning and development, creating an environment where everyone can grow.

## 3. Practice active listening and rephrasing

Active listening goes far beyond simply hearing. It forms the foundation of constructive exchanges and helps create a climate where everyone feels **heard and understood**. Combined with rephrasing, this method turns feedback into genuine learning opportunities.

### The essentials of active listening

Listening actively means giving your **full attention** to the person speaking. This involves paying attention not only to their words but also to body language and tone, in order to grasp all the nuances of the exchange.

Show that you are fully engaged in the conversation by using **visual cues** such as eye contact or nodding. These small gestures strengthen connection and show interest. Also, step away from distractions like phones or computer screens that could break your focus.

> "Active listening requires you to listen attentively to a speaker, understand what they're saying, respond and reflect on what is being said, and retain the information for later."

Attentive listening naturally paves the way for effective and constructive rephrasing.

### Rephrasing: a tool to validate and clarify

Rephrasing means restating, in your own words, what has been expressed. It is a way to verify that you have understood correctly while showing your conversation partner that their perspective is being taken into account. Introduce your rephrasing with phrases like "If I understand correctly..." or "In other words..."

After rephrasing, ask a **confirmation question**: "Is that right?" or "Did I understand your idea correctly?" This gives your conversation partner the opportunity to correct or expand on their point.

Be careful, however, not to simply repeat word for word what was said. The goal is to **interpret and reformulate based on your understanding**, showing that you have absorbed and analyzed the information.

### Smoother and more productive exchanges

Combining active listening and rephrasing can transform the quality of exchanges. It has been shown that **employees feel twice as heard** when their managers take the time to listen and act accordingly. In peer discussions, this strengthens trust and encourages greater openness to dialogue.

Misunderstandings, often the source of conflicts, decrease significantly. Poor communication is responsible for the breakdown of collaboration in more than two-thirds of teams. By adopting these techniques, you create a framework where ideas flow with clarity and precision.

### Practical tips for integrating these techniques

- Ask **clarifying questions** to explore unclear points. For example: "What do you mean by...?" or "Can you illustrate that with an example?"

- Summarize the key points at the end of each exchange to confirm shared understanding.

- Maintain an **open attitude** and suspend all judgment while your conversation partner is speaking. Avoid mentally preparing your response before they have finished. This promotes sincere and attentive listening.

> "Active listening is about understanding the other person first, then being understood as a listener."

By applying these techniques, you foster richer interactions and lay the groundwork for increasingly constructive feedback.

## 4. Focus on observable behaviors, not assumptions

When giving feedback to a colleague, distinguishing what you observe from what you assume can radically change the quality of the exchange. As with active listening, relying on concrete facts strengthens the relevance and effectiveness of feedback. Observable behaviors are actions that can be verified, while assumptions involve judgments about character or intentions.

Focusing your feedback on specific behaviors opens more objective and less emotional discussions. By basing your comments on facts and data, you make your feedback clearer and more constructive. This method also minimizes the risk of your remarks being perceived as personal criticism, which reduces defensive reactions. This aligns with the more neutral and factual communication discussed earlier.

> "Effective feedback focuses on specific and observable behaviors without judgment. Using precise language about practices and examples improves the impact of feedback." - [Edustaff](https://www.edustaff.org/)

### The difference between observation and judgment

> "When you give feedback, are your statements largely observational or judgmental? If you try to pass off a judgment as a fact, you risk being wrong and creating an atmosphere of defensiveness, resistance, or worse."

Consider an example proposed by [BetterUp](https://www.betterup.com/). Rather than saying: "The presenter has no experience facilitating workshops," prefer an observation such as: "The presenter seemed a bit hesitant, and the session didn't really find its rhythm." This type of reformulation emphasizes observable facts, thus avoiding hasty conclusions.

### Practical techniques for staying objective

To integrate this approach into your exchanges, here are some simple techniques to adopt:

- Direct your comments toward specific actions or behaviors rather than personality. For example, start with "I noticed that..." to maintain a respectful and neutral tone.

- Avoid vague terms by choosing precise expressions that directly describe the issue. Talk about the impact of actions rather than making personal judgments.

- Ask questions to understand the reasons behind a behavior, especially if you feel you might be making a judgment.

| **Observable behaviors**                         | **Assumptions**                    |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| "You interrupted three times during the meeting" | "You don't respect others"         |
| "The report was submitted two days late"         | "You're not organized"             |
| "You haven't responded to emails since Tuesday"  | "You don't care about the project" |

Adopting a fact-based approach allows your conversation partner to explain their perspective or intentions. This fosters a constructive exchange rather than a confrontation.

By following these principles, you contribute to creating a climate conducive to open exchanges and continuous improvement.

## 5. Balance criticism with positive reinforcement

Giving constructive feedback means finding the right balance between criticism and recognition of strengths. This not only encourages but also transforms every exchange into an opportunity for improvement.

Research suggests an average ratio of **5.6 positive comments for every piece of criticism**. This figure shows how important it is to highlight successes, even when introducing necessary adjustments. Too much criticism can lead to stress and, ultimately, burnout. Conversely, excessive or insincere praise risks creating complacency that hinders progress.

> "Constructive feedback - both positive and negative - is essential to helping managers enhance their best qualities and address their worst so they can excel at leading." - Harvard Business Review

### Practical techniques for balanced feedback

To apply these principles, use simple methods like the sandwich technique. It consists of starting with positive feedback, then addressing areas for improvement, and concluding on an encouraging note. The key is to formulate **specific and deserved** praise so it retains its impact.

Here are some examples of balanced statements:

- _"You're excellent at problem-solving. It would be great to see this skill showcased more during our team meetings."_

- _"You have a real talent for building relationships. However, sometimes your message isn't entirely clear. Perhaps you could take the time to break your ideas into several steps or explain them more simply."_

### The impact on performance and engagement

The numbers speak for themselves: employees who receive strength-based feedback show **8.9% higher profitability**. Additionally, **79% of employees** who leave their jobs cite a lack of recognition as the primary reason. Teams that practice open and positive communication increase their chances of project success by **47%**.

> "Never assume that an employee knows he/she is doing a good job. Support self-efficacy by 'catching them doing well' and praising their efforts." - Positive Psychology

### Tailor feedback to each individual

Finally, it is crucial to personalize your feedback. Each person has different preferences: some appreciate public praise, while others prefer a private setting. Take the time to observe your colleagues and adapt your approach. Connecting your feedback to each person's personal and professional goals makes it more relevant and motivating. This personalization creates a virtuous cycle, reinforcing continuous improvement and building the foundations for effective communication.

## 6. Adapt feedback to individual preferences

Everyone communicates differently, and adjusting your feedback based on these differences can transform your professional exchanges into genuine tools for growth. This personalized approach complements the structured methods discussed earlier, making your feedback even more effective and constructive.

### Understanding your colleagues' communication styles

To personalize your feedback, start by carefully observing your colleagues. Analyze their body language, facial expressions, and reactions. Some prefer direct feedback based on concrete facts, while others, who are more detail-oriented, favor in-depth explanations. Team members focused on cooperation emphasize human relationships, while those who value influence place great importance on emotional connections.

### How to adapt your feedback in practice

Take a proactive approach by asking questions or conducting surveys to understand their preferences: which channels do they prefer? What frequency of feedback do they expect? Do they prefer a formal or relaxed tone? This demonstrates your willingness to create a respectful environment tailored to each individual.

- With detail-oriented people, provide thorough and precise information.

- For influence-oriented team members, use a warm tone and summarize the key points.

- More reserved individuals will appreciate your patience and the recognition of their contributions.

- With assertive personalities, adopt a confident and engaging tone.

### Keep cultural differences in mind

Cultural differences play a major role in how feedback is perceived. In some cultures, straightforward communication is the norm, while others prefer a more indirect approach to preserve harmony. In a multicultural environment, it is crucial to clarify implicit expectations and openly discuss communication norms.

> "Knowing your personal communication style - and adapting that style to the needs of your team - will help avoid misunderstandings and keep your team operating at peak effectiveness." - Mary Sharp Emerson, Digital Content Producer at Harvard DCE

By adjusting your feedback to individual needs, you foster better cohesion and increased efficiency within your team, while laying the groundwork for continuous improvement.

## 9. Train teams in effective feedback techniques

Even with a well-organized feedback schedule, the quality of exchanges primarily depends on the skills of the participants. Did you know that **63% of employees who receive poor-quality feedback are more likely to leave their position**? This shows how crucial it is to train teams to give and receive feedback constructively. Here are the key elements for building an effective training program.

### The foundations of a good training program

A feedback training program should teach people to express and receive feedback in a professional and constructive manner. Essential topics include:

- Differentiating between positive and negative feedback.

- Using proven models to structure feedback.

- Managing emotions in sometimes sensitive exchanges.

- Adapting the message based on the recipient.

Empathy plays a central role in constructive feedback. Training programs should include exercises that promote active and empathetic listening. This helps managers formulate their feedback in a respectful and clear way while remaining effective.

### The measurable benefits of structured training

The data speaks for itself: **employees who regularly receive feedback are 3 times less likely to look for a new job** and **1.4 times more likely to stay with their current company**. ### Practical exercises: learning by doing

Feedback circles are an excellent exercise. They allow managers to practice giving and receiving feedback in a safe and supportive environment. These sessions help identify the impact of their communication style.

Role-playing exercises are also very useful. For example, simulating situations such as dealing with an authoritarian supervisor, addressing repeated tardiness, or resolving conflicts between colleagues. These scenarios help develop skills for staying calm and effective, even in emotionally sensitive conversations.

### Considering diversity and cultural contexts

In a multicultural environment, cultural misunderstandings can complicate exchanges: **71% of managers consider this a major obstacle in cross-border work**. Effective training should include modules on recognizing cultural biases and encourage open discussions about these differences.

A notable example is **IBM**, which launched global leadership training programs focused on cultural awareness. These initiatives led to a 15% increase in employee satisfaction and retention.
