The world of management is evolving rapidly, and with it, new management methods are emerging to address today's challenges. Role Based Management (RBM) stands out as an innovative approach that enables companies to adopt flexible governance and role management methods. In this article, we dive into the foundations of RBM, explore its advantages, and discuss its practical application.
## What is Role Based Management?

Role Based Management is a management method based on assigning roles within an organization. Rather than focusing solely on positions or titles, RBM focuses on defining functions and responsibilities in terms of nested roles. This approach provides a holistic view of how the company operates and makes it easy to identify the people involved in various activities, whether it's decision-making, meetings, or strategic discussions.
## The Essence of Role Based Management

Simplicity and versatility are at the heart of RBM. It can be adapted to different organizational contexts, from traditional organizations with rigid hierarchies to more collaborative structures like sociocracy or holacracy. RBM draws its inspiration from methods based on circles and roles, such as sociocracy, and aims to make management practices accessible and beneficial for all members of the organization.
## Breaking Down Roles

Roles are at the core of RBM. A role represents a specific organizational function with a clearly defined purpose. Roles can be nested within one another, reflecting the cascading organizational structure. For example, the "HR" role could include the sub-roles "Payroll" and "Interviews", illustrating how roles complement and support each other.
## Role Holders

Each role can be held by one or more members of the organization. If a role has neither sub-roles nor assigned members, it is considered vacant, leaving the possibility to redefine or redistribute its responsibilities.
## Representative Roles

In RBM, a role can be designated as a representative role. This feature allows a role to represent its parent role within another role, often within its parent's parent role. For example, a "Director" role within the "HR" role can represent the human resources department within the organization.
## Participants: Who is Involved?

A role's participants include the members invited to attend meetings and take part in decision-making related to that role. Participants can include:

- Members directly holding the role.
- Members representing sub-roles within the role in question.
- Members of sub-roles that have no representatives.
- Members representing other roles.

## Topics and Tasks: Structuring Action

Topics and tasks are essential elements of RBM for assigning actions and responsibilities to roles. Topics can contain proposals, announcements, questions, or tensions (in the context of holacracy). Tasks define concrete objectives to achieve and can be updated asynchronously or during synchronous meetings.
## Meetings: A Space for Collaboration

Meetings are key moments in RBM. Each meeting is assigned to a specific role, and that role's participants are invited to take part. Meetings are structured in advance and can include topics and tasks that have not yet been addressed. During these meetings, topics are discussed, tasks are updated, and decisions are made while taking each participant's contribution into account.

## The Future of RBM: Customization and Evolution

RBM provides a solid foundation, but it is not set in stone. You can customize this method to meet the specific needs of your organization. Add roles such as Facilitator or Secretary, define recurring meetings with precise agendas, and adapt decision-making processes to your preferences. RBM also allows a smooth transition to other forms of organization, whether it's a traditional hierarchy or more agile methods like holacracy or sociocracy.
