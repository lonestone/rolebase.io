## Roles and Responsibilities During a Meeting

For a meeting to be productive, it is important to assign specific roles to participants. Each role plays a key part in the smooth running of the meeting.

### Meeting Leader

The leader, or facilitator, is responsible for:

- **Directing discussions** following the agenda.

- **Ensuring objectives** are met.

- **Encouraging exchanges** between participants.

- **Maintaining a constructive atmosphere** throughout the session.

### Timekeeper

The timekeeper's mission is to:

- **Ensure timing is respected** for each agenda item.

- **Alert the group** when time is running over.

- **Adjust the schedule** if necessary to maintain a good pace.

### Meeting Secretary

The secretary is responsible for:

- **Recording decisions made** and actions to be taken.

- **Sharing a clear summary** with participants after the meeting.

### Decision-Maker

The decision-maker steps in to:

- **Close debates** and resolve disagreements.

- **Validate proposals** that have been discussed.

- **Confirm commitments** made by the group.

### Team Members

Participants play an active role by:

- **Bringing their expertise** on the topics discussed.

- **Proposing ideas or solutions**.

- **Committing to actions decided** during the meeting.

| Role | Main Responsibility | Impact on Productivity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Leader | Overall coordination | Ensures progress |
| Timekeeper | Schedule adherence | Optimizes duration |
| Secretary | Documenting exchanges | Facilitates follow-up |
| Decision-Maker | Decision-making | Accelerates progress |
| Team Members | Active participation | Enriches discussions |

These roles help structure meetings effectively and ensure a clear distribution of responsibilities. Their strategic assignment will be covered in the following sections.

## How to Assign Roles in Meetings

Assigning roles effectively is crucial for ensuring a meeting runs smoothly. Here is how to proceed.

### Define Meeting Objectives

Clear objectives help identify which roles are needed. For example, a meeting focused on strategic decisions will require an experienced decision-maker, while a brainstorming session will need a facilitator capable of stimulating the group's ideas.

### Identify Key Participants

Choose participants based on their expertise and their ability to contribute to the topics at hand. Limit the number of participants to maintain a fluid and productive discussion. Generally, a group of 5 to 8 people is ideal.

### Match Skills to Roles

Assign roles based on each participant's strengths. Here is a practical guide:

| Participant Profile | Recommended Role | Why? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Experienced manager | Meeting leader | Experience in team coordination |
| Organized person | Timekeeper | Strong sense of planning |
| Methodical collaborator | Secretary | Excellent synthesis skills |
| Expert in a key area | Decision-maker | Deep understanding of the issues |

Inform participants of their roles in advance for optimal preparation.

### Communicate Roles Before the Meeting

Each participant should know their role at least 48 hours before the meeting. This gives them time to prepare and contributes to better efficiency. Clear communication also facilitates regular role rotation, allowing everyone to develop new skills.

### Establish Role Rotation

Regularly changing roles offers several benefits:

- Developing new skills

- Better understanding others' responsibilities

- Avoiding routine

- Strengthening team spirit

To organize this rotation, establish a quarterly calendar. This allows everyone to anticipate their future roles and responsibilities.
